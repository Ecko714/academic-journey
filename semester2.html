  • CHM 113 - General Chemistry I

    • Course Description

    "This course will cover the nature of atoms and elemental substances, the combination of atoms to form molecules and compounds, the interactions between atoms and molecules, chemical bonding models, relationships between chemical and physical properties, thermochemistry, and solutions. More importantly, you will have the opportunity to practice your critical thinking skills and learn how the macroscopic world around you can be explained by the microscopic world underneath."

    No thank you. I would not like to relive the truama that I had to endure to pass this class. Nope. I'm good. Interesting stuff but... no.

  • CSE 205 - Object-Oriented Program & Data

    • Course Description

    "Problem solving by using an object-oriented programming language. Introduction to data structures."

    Ok! Another CS course. This one was good, I liked this one. Here, students learned the basics of data structures which are essentially different ways to store data in any application. You've got:

    • Arrays
    • Linked Lists
    • Trees
    • Stacks (Very Important!)
    • ... and many more!

    This class also gave a taste of how to program GUIs (Graphical User Interfaces) with JavaFX. Very interesting at the time.

  • ENG 102 - First-Year Composition

    • Course Description

    "This course is designed to allow students to learn about the processes of communicating effectively in writing, that is, with purpose and towards specific goals."

    This class was the second required half of the English course. Same professor, a slight change in material but still an english class. You write essays and create creative pieces. We know how I feel.

  • MAT 265 - Calculus for Engineers I

    • Course Description

    "In this course, we will focus on limits and continuity, differential calculus of functions of one variable, and an introduction to integration."

    And here starts the downfall of my steady academic career! Oh boy. This was the first time I've run into actual calculus. I've heard about it (obviously pre-calculus implied what was coming) but I've never taken the time to look into what the math was for. I knew that Algebra focused on variables and Geometry on shapes but what in the world was calculus for?

    Well, as the description says, differerential calculus is used to study how functions change with respect to a single variable. You learn how to find the limit of a function, it's derivative, and eventually you get a taste on how to find it's integral. I thought I was good at math but for some reason, calculus never made total sense to me. That is until later... we'll get to that. Combine an older professor with a heavy accent, a sloppy blackboard full of chalk, and the beginning of the coronavirus and you get a recipe for a failing grade.

    ANYWAY, here's some random homework about finding limits (I know it's kinda hard to see, my bad).

  • TCL 101 - Latinas/os in the U.S.

    • Course Description

    "This course focuses on contemporary issues related to Latino populations across the United States. We will discuss the experiences of diverse Latino groups, including Puerto Ricans, Cubans, Dominicans, Salvadorans, and others. Through readings, class discussion, videos, and other activities, we will examine contemporary issues such as racial and ethnic identities, international migration, politics, employment, globalization, and cultural changes, to name but a few."

    One of the first classes that I can call my favorite! This was such a joy to take. This course dived deep into the history of Latinx culture in the United States. We started with understanding the sub-cultures of Hispanics, Latinos, Chicanos, and Boricuas and ended with Latinx politics. Growing up as a Hispanic, the only time I was ever able to celebrate my culture was when I was with family. I didn't have many other friends that shared my culture so being able to take this class to really dive deeper into what it means to be Hispanic was very fulfilling. We wrote essays, had discussions, and watched movies to have conversations about different histories.
